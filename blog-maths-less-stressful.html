Parent Support

Making maths and science feel less stressful

Small changes in the way students learn can replace pressure with curiosity, confidence and steady progress.

Maths and science can feel stressful when a student believes that every answer must come quickly or that one mistake means they are not good at the subject. The truth is that both subjects are learned step by step. With clear explanations, regular practice and encouragement, students can feel far more comfortable asking questions and trying again.

Start with understanding, not memorising

Formulas, definitions and diagrams are easier to remember when students understand what they mean. In maths, ask what each step is doing. In science, connect a new idea to something visible in everyday life, such as motion, light, plants or electricity. Concept clarity gives students a dependable starting point when a question looks unfamiliar.

Break difficult topics into smaller wins

A long chapter can feel overwhelming, so focus on one skill at a time. A maths session might cover only fractions or one type of equation. A science session might focus on one diagram, definition or experiment. Completing small tasks builds momentum and makes revision feel achievable.

Make mistakes part of learning

Mistakes show exactly where support is needed. Instead of simply correcting an answer, encourage students to find the first step where they got stuck and try a similar question. Keeping a small “mistake notebook” for common calculation errors, units or definitions turns errors into useful revision points.

Use a calm, repeatable practice routine

  • Spend 10 minutes reviewing one concept in your own words.
  • Solve a few questions, starting with an easier example.
  • Check the method, not only the final answer.
  • Write down one doubt to ask a teacher or tutor.

Short, regular practice helps students meet questions before exam day. It is much less stressful than trying to complete an entire chapter at the last minute.

Praise effort, questions and progress

Confidence grows when students hear that effort matters. Praise a thoughtful question, a neatly shown method or the courage to attempt a difficult problem. At home and in class, calm feedback helps students see maths and science as skills they can improve, not subjects they have to fear.

Why this helps

  • Builds confidence through manageable practice
  • Improves concept clarity and problem-solving
  • Reduces pressure around homework and exams
